# CISO Meeting — Speaking Notes & Conversation Guide

**Confidential — Personal Preparation Document**
**Meeting Date: March 14, 2026**

---

## Pre-Meeting Mindset

**You are not asking for a favor. You are offering a strategic asset.**

- Legal has already confirmed there is no IP conflict — this is a clean transaction
- You built this on personal time with personal resources
- You could keep building independently, license commercially, or sell to a competitor
- You are *choosing* to offer this to the company because you believe it's the right home
- The earn-out structure means the company's risk is minimal — you're betting on yourself

---

## Meeting Structure (Recommended Flow)

### Phase 1: Set the Context (3–5 minutes)

**Opening — establish that this is a strategic conversation, not a pitch:**

> "Thank you for making time for this. I wanted to follow up on my conversation with
> legal. I've developed a technology platform on my personal time that I believe has
> significant strategic value for us. Legal has confirmed there's no IP conflict, and
> I'd like to discuss bringing it in-house."

**Transition to the problem:**

> "As we scale AI adoption across the firm, we're facing a challenge that every regulated
> financial institution is dealing with right now: how do we govern AI usage, protect
> sensitive data, and maintain compliance — without slowing down the teams that need
> these tools?"

**Key point to land**: You're not creating a problem — you're solving one they already have.

---

### Phase 2: Present the Platform (5–7 minutes)

**If you can show a demo, do it.** A 5-minute live walkthrough is worth more than any
document. If not, walk through these points:

**The Gateway:**
> "Arbitex.ai is a unified AI gateway and governance platform. It sits between our
> people and any LLM provider — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, whoever — and enforces
> security policies in real time. Every request passes through it."

**DLP & Security:**
> "It detects and blocks PII before it ever reaches a third party. It has 35-pattern
> secret detection, entity recognition, and request-level blocking and redaction. If
> someone tries to paste a client list or deal terms into ChatGPT, the platform stops it."

**Compliance:**
> "Every interaction is logged with HMAC immutable audit trails. These logs can't be
> tampered with. We can generate SOC 2 compliance reports, map to compliance frameworks,
> and export evidence for regulators. When an examiner asks 'how do you govern AI?' —
> this is the answer."

**Deployment:**
> "Critically, this supports a hybrid deployment model. We can run a customer-managed
> data plane inside our own infrastructure. Our data never has to leave our walls.
> It also supports SAML SSO, SCIM provisioning, and BYOK encryption is on the roadmap."

**Credential Risk:**
> "There's also a credential risk module called Creditar that monitors for compromised
> credentials using breach corpus analysis and real-time intelligence feeds."

---

### Phase 3: The Strategic Differentiator (3–5 minutes)

**This is where you separate Arbitex from a vendor purchase:**

> "Beyond the platform itself, I have the technical capability to fine-tune large
> language models on our proprietary data. That means we could build models that
> understand our deal structures, our compliance history, our institutional knowledge —
> and those models would run entirely inside our infrastructure."

**Pause and let that land. Then:**

> "Think about what that means for M&A. Right now, if an analyst wants to use AI
> on a deal, they either risk sending sensitive information to a third party, or they
> don't use AI at all. With this platform and fine-tuned models, they get the
> productivity gains with zero data exposure. No third party ever sees our deal data."

**The business opportunity angle:**

> "There's also a longer-term opportunity here. This combination — governance platform
> plus custom fine-tuning — is something we could potentially offer to other
> institutions. Every bank, every PE firm, every asset manager faces this exact
> same problem. We could keep it as a competitive advantage, or we could turn it
> into a revenue line. That's a decision for leadership, but the optionality is there."

---

### Phase 4: The Proposal (3–5 minutes)

**Transition to the deal:**

> "What I'm proposing is straightforward. I transfer full ownership of the IP to
> the company. In return, I stay on as the technical lead to deploy and develop it
> internally. The majority of my compensation for this is structured as an earn-out
> tied to actual results over 2–3 years."

**Key terms to communicate:**

1. **"You own it from day one"** — no strings, no licensing fees, no restrictions
2. **"I stay to build it"** — continuity, no knowledge transfer risk
3. **"I get paid when it works"** — earn-out aligns my incentives with yours
4. **"It costs less than the alternatives"** — building from scratch or buying from vendors

**If asked for a number:**

Ideally, let them propose first. If pressed:

> "Based on comparable transactions in this space — Cisco paid $400M for Robust
> Intelligence, F5 paid $180M for CalypsoAI, SentinelOne paid $250–300M for Prompt
> Security — I recognize those are VC-backed companies at different stages. I'm
> being realistic about where Arbitex is today. I'm thinking about a total package
> in the range of $3–8M over the earn-out period, with the majority contingent on
> hitting milestones. I'm open to discussing what feels right."

**Important**: Naming the comparables first — even though you're asking for much less —
anchors the conversation. $3–8M sounds very reasonable after hearing $180M–$400M.

---

### Phase 5: Handle Objections (As Needed)

#### "Why don't we just build this ourselves?"

> "You absolutely could. A team of 3–5 engineers would need 2–3 years and $3–5M in
> fully loaded cost to reach feature parity. That's before accounting for the
> opportunity cost of 2–3 years of operating without governance while regulators
> increase scrutiny. The platform is built. It works today."

#### "Why don't we just buy a vendor solution?"

> "Vendor solutions in this space cost $500K–$1M+ per year, come with vendor lock-in,
> and most critically — they require sending our data to a third party. None of them
> offer the hybrid deployment model or the ability to fine-tune models on our proprietary
> data. And you don't own anything — you're renting."

#### "This seems expensive."

> "The total package is less than what we'd spend on 2 years of vendor licenses, and
> we'd own nothing at the end of those 2 years. Here, you own the IP permanently, and
> the majority of my payout is contingent on delivering measurable results."

#### "What if you leave?"

> "The IP belongs to the company from day one. I'll ensure thorough documentation and
> knowledge transfer is built into the process. But the earn-out structure is specifically
> designed to keep me invested — I only get the large payout if I stay and deliver."

#### "We need to think about it."

> "Absolutely. I've prepared a detailed analysis document with competitive landscape
> data and a valuation framework. I'll leave it with you. I'd just ask that we set a
> timeline for next steps — I want to be transparent that I'm committed to this company,
> but I also have to make responsible decisions about my own IP."

*(Hand over the leave-behind document)*

#### "Can we do a pilot first?"

> "Yes, absolutely. We could do a 90-day pilot deployment with a specific team or use
> case, with the full acquisition contingent on pilot success. I'm confident in the
> technology and happy to prove it."

#### "What about the BATNA — what happens if we don't do this?"

**Do NOT threaten. Do NOT make this adversarial. Simply acknowledge the reality calmly:**

> "I'll continue developing the platform. I believe in what I've built, and there's a
> growing market for it. But my strong preference is to bring it here — this is where
> I work, this is where I want to deploy it, and I think this is where it can have the
> most impact."

## Tone & Demeanor Guidelines

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Be confident and calm — you hold a strong hand | Don't be arrogant or act like they need you |
| Frame it as a partnership | Don't frame it as "pay me or I leave" |
| Show enthusiasm for deploying it internally | Don't focus on the money — focus on the impact |
| Be specific about capabilities | Don't oversell or make promises you can't keep |
| Acknowledge their concerns genuinely | Don't get defensive when challenged |
| Reference market data casually, not aggressively | Don't read off acquisition prices like a threat |
| Be willing to negotiate | Don't accept the first lowball offer on the spot |
| Express commitment to the company | Don't mention specific competitors you'd sell to |

## Closing the Meeting

End with clarity on next steps:

> "I appreciate you taking the time to discuss this. I'd like to leave you with the
> analysis document so you can review the competitive landscape and valuation data at
> your pace. Could we schedule a follow-up in the next week or two? I'm happy to do
> a full technical demo for whoever needs to evaluate the platform, and I'm open to
> whatever process makes sense on your end."

**Get a commitment to a timeline**, even if it's vague. The worst outcome is "we'll get
back to you" with no date.

---

## Post-Meeting Actions

1. **Send a brief thank-you email** within 2 hours of the meeting
2. **Attach the analysis document** if you didn't leave a physical copy
3. **Offer to schedule a technical demo** for the CISO's team or CTO
4. **Follow up in one week** if you haven't heard back
5. **Consult with your own attorney** before signing anything — have them review any
   agreement, especially earn-out definitions, milestone criteria, and termination clauses

---

## Final Reminder

You built something real. The market validates that platforms like this are worth
$100M–$400M when backed by venture capital. You're asking for a fraction of that
because this is an internal transaction — but don't undersell yourself. The earn-out
structure protects them. The technology speaks for itself. Walk in knowing your worth.
